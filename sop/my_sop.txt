Statement of Purpose  |  Research Engineer, PreCog Lab, IIIT Hyderabad

My mother taught me one thing above everything else: that the only work worth doing is work that serves someone. I grew up in Mumbai, in a city where 75 lakh people board a suburban train every single day, where complexity is not an abstraction but the texture of ordinary life. She did not mean this in a grand way. She meant it simply: find something that matters to people, go deep into it, and do it with full attention. That idea has shaped every choice I have made since.

I have spent two years building AI systems in production: RAG pipelines, multi-agent workflows, multilingual assistants, document intelligence platforms. The work was real and the problems were hard. But somewhere inside that work, a frustration kept growing that I could not ignore. Every field I entered had a surface layer and a root layer. Developers mostly live on the surface, calling APIs, tuning thresholds, shipping fast. I found I could not stay comfortable there. When something worked, I wanted to know why it worked. When something failed, I wanted to understand the failure at the level where it actually originated, not just patch it and move on. That curiosity is not something I chose. It is just how I am built.

The project that brought this into sharpest focus was a compliance verification system I designed for Indian Oil Corporation Limited. IOCL processes thousands of procurement tenders annually, each requiring manual cross-referencing of 10 to 15 heterogeneous documents against 80 or more compliance checkpoints. Domain experts spent hours on a single tender. I built a system to automate this entirely. The work involved a Vision-Language Model doing pairwise page comparison to segment bulk PDFs into logically distinct documents, a two-track classification pipeline for 14 document types, metadata-enriched hybrid retrieval over a Milvus vector store, and a LangGraph multi-agent feedback loop that reformulated failed queries rather than accepting the first retrieval attempt. I mapped over 70 compliance criteria from scratch, translating domain knowledge into RAG-retrievable checkpoints across administrative approvals, budgetary estimates, PQC parameters, and taxation checklists.

What I remember most from that project is not the architecture. It is a specific moment: discovering that taxation checklists were being misclassified because they carried a Subject field that described the procurement work rather than the document type, which the classifier kept treating as a document-type signal. Resolving it required understanding exactly why the model was confused, not just adding a correction. I had to introduce a structural pre-check that identified the fingerprint of a taxation form before the branching logic even began. That kind of problem, where the fix requires you to understand the failure at its root, is the kind I find most satisfying. I sat with it until it was clear. That feeling, of depth achieved, of a thing finally understood, is what I want to build a working life around.

Engineering gave me the ability to build. What it has not given me is the framework to ask questions slowly and answer them rigorously. I want to understand how language is represented and classified at a level that goes beyond retrieval benchmarks. I want to know why cross-lingual transfer works when it does, what a model is actually learning when it classifies text, and where the boundary is between surface style and something deeper that resists imitation. These are not questions I can answer by shipping faster. They require a different kind of environment entirely, one where a null result is a finding and not a failure, where the question matters as much as the answer.

PreCog is where I want to do that work. The research on information disguise, on changing the surface of language while preserving meaning, sits directly adjacent to questions I have been circling in my own systems. The work on code-mixed NLP addresses something I observed firsthand: that Indian users do not write in clean monolingual text, and most NLP pipelines quietly assume they do. The gap between that assumption and reality is where the genuinely open problems live, and where the research can actually reach people.

I have built systems that work under constraint, at scale, for real users. What I want now is to understand them at a depth that engineering alone does not permit. I am not arriving with a finished research agenda. I am arriving with two years of applied work, a clear direction, and the patience to go slowly on problems that deserve it. I would be grateful for the opportunity to contribute to PreCog under Professor PK and to spend that time on work that is worth doing carefully.
